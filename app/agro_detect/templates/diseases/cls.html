CERCOSPORA LEAF SPOT OF COWPEA

Symptoms

  • First symptoms are leaves having inconspicuous spots, reddish-brown- up to15mm diameter, not restricted by veins.
  • Leaf spots merge as leaves go yellow and die.
  • Dead areas on the leaf may fall out giving a shot-hole appearance.
  • The disease flourishes on older leaves.
  • The disease is caused by the fungus, Mycosphaerella cruenta

Prevention
  1. Use less susceptible cowpea varieties (e.g. slipea 1, 2 and 3; VRB-10)
  2. Use clean seed; either obtain the seed from a reliable seed supplier or clean the seed of plant debris.
  3. Plant new crop seed and not from older ones with disease.
  4. Use healthy, and disease-free seeds
  5. Use correct planting density (wider spacing such as 50 cm x 30 cm or 50 cm x 40 cm)
  6. Intercrop cowpea with maize or sorghum in order to reduce spread of the disease
  7. Remove alternative host plants (e.g. Ageratum conyzoides)
  8. Remove volunteer plants of cowpea and other legumes before field planting.
  9. Remove weeds at 3 and 6 weeks after planting
  10. Plough and harrow to destroy fungal inoculum in the soil
  11. Plough under crop residues
  12. Crop rotation (cowpea + maize)
  13. Destroy diseased debris
  14. Avoiding excessive irrigation is recommended

Direct control
  • Remove diseased plants and burn outside the crop field, burn or bury the trash after harvest.
  • Remove heavily infected plants to prevent spread of inoculum
  • Early pruning of infected leaves to prevent further spread of inoculum
  • Spray aqueous leaf extracts from neem and pepper (leaf powder: 100 g/ litres water). Apply early in the morning or late in the evening; Do not apply when it is raining or windy; Use protective gears to avoid being poisoned.
  • Seed treatment with Mancozeb (ethylene Bisdithiocarbamate): 80g a.i/kg of seeds.
  • Foliage treatment with Mancozeb fungicide: 100 ml/14 L of water. This should be applied after the crop has flowered and pods are starting to develop, with a maximum of 2-3 applications per planting season, though its cost-effectiveness is doubtful.
  • Apply Cupric oxide (Copper oxide) on cowpea foliage
